⏺ Interactive hero — make the landing page itself feel like a drift

  - "Where would you drift right now?" — a single input in the hero. You type a
  city neighborhood (or just a vibe: "I want coffee and old buildings") and it
  shows a teaser — not a full route, just the first stop, a photo, and a
  "generate full drift →" CTA that leads to the demo. Turns the landing page

  - Day/night hero shift — detect visitor's local time. If it's evening, the
  hero imagery and copy shift: "the city looks different after 8pm." Different
  stops surface in the teaser, different mood.

  Scroll storytelling

  - One-stop cinematic scroll — as you scroll down, you're "walking" through a
  single stop. The neighborhood name fades in, then a photo, then the
  description types itself out, then you hear (optional) a 10-second ambient
  audio clip of the street. Completely immersive. One stop, perfectly told,
  sells the whole product.
  - The tourist vs. the drifter — a split-screen section that animates as you
  scroll. Left side: the tourist itinerary (Eiffel Tower → Louvre → tourist café
   near the Louvre). Right side: a Drift route for the same city, in the same

⏺ Honest take: build a React Native app with Expo. Here's why and how to think
  about it.

  Why Expo specifically
  - You're already in Next.js/TypeScript — Expo is the same mental model, same
  ecosystem
  - Expo Go lets you test on a real phone instantly, no Xcode/Android Studio
  setup
  - Your existing API routes (/api/generate-route, /api/stop-photo) work as-is —
   the app just calls them
  - Expo Router mirrors Next.js App Router almost exactly, so the learning curve
   is minimal

  What the app actually needs that the web demo doesn't
  - GPS — "start from my current location" instead of typing an address. This
  alone makes the product feel completely different
  - Background audio — the AI companion narration playing while your phone is in
   your pocket
  - Offline-capable stop cards — download the route before you leave wifi, view
  it underground
  - Haptics — subtle vibration when you arrive near a stop. Feels premium, costs
   nothing
  - Push notifications — "you're 80m from your next stop" via geofencing

  The honest build order

  1. Core loop first — city picker → vibe selector → generate route → map with
  stops. Get this working and feeling good. Everything else is a feature on top
  of a working product.
  2. GPS start location — replace the text input with a "start from here"

❯ what would the process to add this app to app store would look like?          

⏺ Honest overview — it's more bureaucracy than code.

  Step 1: Apple Developer Account
  - Go to developer.apple.com and enroll — $99/year
  - Takes 24-48h to get approved (sometimes instant, sometimes days)
  - You need this before anything else

  Step 2: Build the app with EAS
  Expo has a build service called EAS (Expo Application Services) that compiles
  your app into a proper .ipa file without you needing Xcode or a Mac build
  machine.
  npm install -g eas-cli
  eas login
  eas build:configure
  eas build --platform ios
  It builds in the cloud, takes ~10-15 minutes. You get a download link.

  Step 3: App Store Connect
  - Create your app listing at appstoreconnect.apple.com
  - Fill in: name, description, keywords, category, age rating, privacy policy
  URL (required)
  - Upload screenshots — they need to be exact pixel dimensions for specific
  device sizes (this is annoying)

  Step 4: Submit for review
  - Upload your build via EAS: eas submit --platform ios
  - Apple reviews it — usually 1-3 days for a first submission, faster after
  that
  - They check for: crashes, broken functionality, misleading descriptions,
  missing privacy disclosures

  Things that will slow you down
  - Privacy policy — required, no exceptions. You need a hosted URL. Even a
  simple one.
  - Location permission — since the app uses GPS, Apple will ask what you do
  with that data. You need to declare it accurately.
  - API calls to your backend — if the app doesn't work in their review
  environment (e.g. your API is down), they reject it
  - Screenshots — need to be real, not placeholder. Required for iPhone 6.5" and
   5.5" at minimum.

  Realistic timeline
  - Developer account approved: 1-2 days
  - Build + listing ready: 1-2 days of work
  - First review: 2-5 days
  - Total: ~1 week if everything goes smoothly, 2-3 weeks if you get rejected
  and need to fix things

  Before you bother with the App Store though — worth testing on your own device
   first via TestFlight (Apple's beta distribution). Same build process, but you
   skip the full review. You can share it with up to 100 people with just an
  email invite. Good for showing it to people before committing to a public
  launch.

  The order I'd do it:
  1. Get the app working well on your phone via Expo Go first
  2. Create the developer account now (so it's ready)
  3. When it's solid, build with EAS + distribute via TestFlight
  4. App Store only when you're actually ready to launch

⏺ Honest answer — here's what VCs and accelerators actually look at, roughly in
  order of weight.

  What you already have (good)
  - Working product (the demo) — this is huge, most applicants have a deck and a
   dream
  - Clear brand and positioning
  - Defined market (cities as B2B customers)
  - Specific launch markets (Warsaw, Berlin, Prague)

  ---
  What's missing and actually matters

  Traction / numbers
  This is the #1 thing. Everything else is secondary. You need something —
  waitlist signups, demo generations, time-on-site, anything real. Even 200
  waitlist emails is better than zero. VCs want to see that someone other than
  you cares.

  - Add analytics (Vercel has built-in, or PostHog free tier) so you can say "X
  unique visitors, Y routes generated, Z minutes average session"
  - Start counting demo generations server-side — log every /api/generate-route
  call so you know how many routes have been made
  - If you have any waitlist signups already, know the number

  A pitch deck
  10-12 slides. Not on the website, but you need it ready:
  - Problem / insight
  - Solution
  - How it works (3 screenshots)
  - Market size (tourism is $10T+ globally — easy to frame)
  - Business model (cities pay, tourists free)
  - Traction
  - Team
  - Ask

  Team
  Solo founders get funded but it's harder. If you have anyone else involved — a
   technical co-founder, an advisor who runs a city tourism board, anyone — put
  them on the site. Even an advisors section with one credible person changes
  the perception dramatically.

  The business model, made concrete
  Right now "cities pay us" is the pitch but there's no pricing anywhere. You
  don't need public pricing, but you need a clear answer to "what does a city
  actually pay and for what?" — even a rough model. €5k/month per city? Revenue
  share on bookings? Sponsorship of stops? Pick one and be able to defend it.

  One real city relationship
  A single email from a Warsaw tourism board saying "we're interested in
  piloting this" is worth more than 50 slides. If you can get any institutional
  contact — tourism board, city marketing office, a hotel group, a neighborhood
  association — pursue it now. Even informal interest counts.

  ---
  Website-specific things that help

  - Metrics bar — "X routes generated · Y cities · Z explorers" somewhere
  visible. Even if the numbers are small, showing them signals you're measuring
  - A "for cities" landing page — you have a ForCities section but a dedicated
  /cities page with a contact form would let you capture B2B inbound. VCs want
  to see you're working both sides
  - Press / social proof — if anyone has written about you, linked to you,
  tweeted about the demo, put it there. If not, get one person to write
  something (a travel blogger, a local newsletter)
  - Team page — even just you, with a real photo and 2 sentences. Anonymity
  reads as amateur

  ---
  Accelerator-specific
  YC, Antler, and most EU accelerators (Seedcamp, EF) care most about: founders
  > idea > traction. In that order. They're betting on you as much as the
  product. For EU-focused ones (relevant given Warsaw/Prague), look at:
  - Antler — Warsaw office exists, very founder-friendly at pre-idea stage
  - Google for Startups — has travel/city tech tracks
  - EIT Urban Mobility — EU-funded, specifically cities + mobility + tourism

  The honest bottom line: get 10 real people to use the demo and tell you what
  they think. That feedback, even informal, is more valuable than any website
  change right now. One good user story ("I used this in Prague and found a bar
  I never would have found") is a slide.
